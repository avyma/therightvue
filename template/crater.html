Meteor Crater and 198 Named Craters (Land)

University of Kentucky Geography

The Meteor Crater and 198 Named Craters map was created to fulfill the requirements put forward by the Wild Eye Adventures - a national outdoor guiding service. Touted as the best preserved impact crater in the world, the Meteor Crater is located in Cococino County, Arizona (approximately 37 miles east of Flagstaff). At an elevation of 5,640 ft above sea level, the Meteor Crater has a diameter of approximately 3,900 ft and an approximate depth of 560 ft. The circumference of the crater rises approximately 148 ft above the surrounding plains. Despite it being an important geological landmark of national stature, the Meteor Crater is privately-owned by the Barringer family through their Barringer Crater Company. Thus, it is not protected as a national monument. However, it did receive the National Natural Landmark designation in 1967.

This map is to serve as an example site map showcasing craters as a landform. In accordance with the requirements, the map of the Meteor Crater, when zoomed in, shows elevation contours (at 20ft intervals). However, when zoomed out, the map shows all other land craters in the country without the elevation contours.

The list of domestic names of craters and related informations was extracted from the Domestic and Antarctic Names (State and Topical Gazetteer) published by the United States Board on Geographic Names (geonames.usgs.gov). According to the Board of Geographic Names, crater as a feature class is defined to include, "Circular-shaped depression at the summit of a volcanic cone or one on the surface of the land caused by the impact of a meteorite; a manmade depression caused by an explosion (caldera, lua). Contours were extracted from a USGS NED 1/3 arc-second raster digital elevation model. This data can be accessed at the USGS 3D Elevation Program (usgs.gov). All data were extracted in December 2019.

Tools used in this project

  • PostGIS was used to store/query the national dataset.
  • Data is shown on Mapbox web map with geolocation services enabled. If the map got popular it would require a fee to use.
  • GDAL and QGIS 3.10 were used to convert raster elevation units and create contours.
